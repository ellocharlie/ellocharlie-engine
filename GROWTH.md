# ellocharlie Growth Strategy
## 7% Week-over-Week from 2 Customers

*April 2026*

---

## The Premise

Paul Graham said "do things that don't scale." He didn't mean it as a phase you move through. He meant it as a discipline — a commitment to customer obsession that is so intense in the early days that it becomes the DNA of the product and the company.

This document is the growth playbook for ellocharlie. It starts with 2 customers and a 7% week-over-week compound growth target. It maps every phase of acquisition and retention from Day 1 to $10M ARR. And it takes Paul Graham's advice seriously: the early work is personal, manual, and irreplaceable. That's where the flywheel starts.

## Phase 1: The First 10 Customers (Weeks 0–24)

This is the "do things that don't scale" phase. The goal is not efficiency. The goal is signal. What do customers actually need? What makes them stay? What makes them call their founder friends and say "you need to try this"?

At 2 customers, you have the rarest and most valuable asset in product development: direct access to every person using your product. We will use it.

### Acquisition Channels

**1. Personal Network — The Warmest Possible Leads**

Nicholas spent eight years building ShipHero into a WMS trusted by thousands of e-commerce brands. Those founders and operators know him. They trust his judgment on software. They are the exact ICP for ellocharlie: operators who have built something real, who have felt the pain of disconnected tools, who will give honest feedback and refer generously if the product earns it.

Beyond ShipHero: the Code and Theory network (senior leaders at major brands and agencies), the NBPA network (sports business executives and operators), the Digitas/Publicis network (enterprise marketing leaders who have gone on to found companies), and Packiyo customers (WMS operators who understand operational infrastructure).

Cristine's network is complementary and non-overlapping: tech veterans from IBM and fast-moving startups, enterprise technology executives who have watched companies fail because they chose tools designed for Fortune 500 procurement processes, and founders she has connected with across her career in the final mile of technology adoption.

The first 10 customers should come entirely from these networks. Not cold outreach. Warm introductions. Real conversations about real problems. The kind where you already know the person's situation before you pitch, because you worked with them for three years.

**2. Build-in-Public Content — The Slow Burn That Pays Later**

The Growth Agent produces three blog posts per week, starting now. Not thought leadership about CRM trends. Documentation of what we're actually building: how we configured the CX Lead Agent, what our health scoring model looks like, how we handle the handoff from sales to onboarding inside ellocharlie itself.

The open-source agent framework goes on GitHub. The README is written for founders who want to build the same kind of agent-driven company. The GitHub stars are not the metric. The conversations that start because a developer saw the repo and got curious — those are the metric.

This content does not convert in week 1. It builds search equity, authority, and trust over months. Start it immediately and let it compound.

**3. Slack Communities — Be Useful, Not Promotional**

Target communities: Indie Hackers, SaaS founders Slacks, startup operator communities, YC alumni channels (where accessible), product-led growth communities. The rule is simple: never post about ellocharlie. Only answer questions about customer operations, CRM setup, onboarding workflows, support systems. Become the person in the community who knows this domain cold.

When someone asks "what CRM should I use for my 5-person startup," the answer is not a link to ellocharlie.com. The answer is a detailed, thoughtful breakdown of the trade-offs. If ellocharlie is genuinely the right answer, we mention it at the end. The reputation is built in the answer, not the pitch.

**4. White-Glove Onboarding**

Nicholas personally onboards every one of the first 10 customers.

This is not scalable. It is also not optional. The first 10 customers are not revenue. They are the product intelligence that makes the next 90 possible. Every question they ask during onboarding becomes a documentation article. Every workflow they struggle to configure becomes a feature. Every piece of feedback they give goes directly into the product intelligence system.

The protocol: one-hour setup call, custom configuration, daily Slack check-in for the first week, weekly check-in call for the first 90 days. For 10 customers, this is 10 hours of onboarding plus 10 weekly calls per month. It's a lot. It's worth every minute.

### Retention in Phase 1

- **CX Lead Agent monitors every interaction** — no customer goes 48 hours without some kind of touchpoint
- **15-minute first-response SLA** — this is the standard from day one
- **Feature requests** go directly into the product intelligence system and are acknowledged within 24 hours
- **Nicholas reviews every support ticket personally** — not to respond to all of them, but to know what's happening in the product

The churn target in Phase 1 is zero. Not as a KPI. As an obsession.

## Phase 2: 10 to 100 Customers (Weeks 24–58)

The "starting to scale" phase. The personal touch doesn't disappear — it evolves. The systems that Nicholas built manually in Phase 1 start to automate. The content that the Growth Agent has been producing since Week 1 starts to compound.

### Acquisition Channels

**1. Content Flywheel**

By Week 24, the Growth Agent has published roughly 70 blog posts. Some of them will be ranking. The ones that rank get doubled down on: expanded, updated, internally linked, and used as anchors for new content clusters.

Target keyword categories:
- "CRM for startups" and all long-tail variants
- "customer onboarding software" + startup/SaaS variants
- "helpdesk for small teams" comparisons
- "CRM + helpdesk combined" (a gap in most comparison content)
- "how to onboard SaaS customers" (top-of-funnel)
- Competitor comparisons (HubSpot, Intercom, Zendesk for small teams)

SEO compounds. The articles published in Week 4 that are ranking in Week 30 will still be ranking in Week 80. Start the clock.

**2. Customer Referrals — The "Tell 3 Founders" Program**

For every customer who reaches a defined activation milestone (first workflow automated, first ticket resolved under SLA, first onboarding completed without manual intervention), they receive an invitation to refer three other founders.

The incentive is not a discount. Discounts commoditize the product and attract price-sensitive customers who churn. The incentive is early access to the next major feature — which has two effects: it generates referrals, and it creates a group of customers who feel like insiders. Insider customers stay.

**3. Integrations as Distribution**

Every integration ellocharlie builds is a distribution channel. When we integrate with Stripe, every Stripe-using startup that searches "CRM that connects to Stripe" is a potential customer. When we publish a widget SDK, every developer who embeds the live chat in their product is a distribution node.

The integration roadmap is a growth document, not just a product document. Prioritize integrations by the size of the user base they unlock.

**4. Startup Program**

Six months free for pre-seed and seed-stage startups. No catch. No credit card required. The loss leader economics are simple: a startup that starts on ellocharlie at 5 customers and grows to 50 customers is worth the 6 months of free service many times over. The retention engine at that scale is the product itself — if we've done our job, they don't have a reason to leave.

The program is also a marketing vehicle. "Backed by YC, uses ellocharlie" becomes a signal in the startup community in the same way that "we use Stripe" became a signal of technical credibility in 2012.

**5. Open-Source Community → Product Awareness**

GitHub stars on the agent framework are a leading indicator of developer awareness, which converts to founder awareness. The developers building startups in 2026 are the founders evaluating CRM tools in 2027. Build the community now.

**6. Case Studies**

Every one of the first 10 customers becomes a detailed case study. Not a PR piece. A real case study: the problem they had before ellocharlie, the specific configuration they built, the metrics before and after. The Growth Agent writes the first draft; the CEO approves every word.

These case studies serve three purposes: they give prospective customers a proof point that's credible because it's specific; they make the existing customer feel valued and visible; and they create content that ranks for very specific long-tail queries ("CRM for e-commerce startup," "helpdesk for SaaS with 3-person team").

### Retention in Phase 2

- **Health scoring** automatically identifies accounts that are at risk based on usage patterns, support ticket volume, and login frequency. The CX Lead Agent surfaces these to Cristine before the customer knows they're unhappy.
- **Automated onboarding workflows** reduce time-to-value from days to hours. The customer who reaches their first activation milestone in under 24 hours is dramatically more likely to stay.
- **Knowledge base with AI search** reduces tier-1 support load by giving customers the answer before they open a ticket.
- **Product intelligence** — every feature request from every customer feeds into a prioritization model. Customers who see their requests shipped stay. This is the most underrated retention lever in SaaS.

## Phase 3: 100 to 1,000+ Customers (Weeks 58–92)

The "product-led growth" phase. At this point, the growth engine is no longer driven by Nicholas and Cristine personally — it's driven by the product, the content, and the community working in concert.

### Acquisition Channels

**1. Self-Serve Motion**

The 7-day free trial converts without human touch for a growing percentage of customers. The onboarding sequence, written and maintained by the Growth Agent, walks a new user from signup to first value without a call. The product does the selling.

The key metric: Trial-to-paid conversion rate. Target: >20%. Levers: in-product prompts, activation milestone design, day-3 check-in sequence (automated, personal in tone), removal of friction in the upgrade flow.

**2. Product-Qualified Leads**

Users who hit defined activation milestones — first automation built, first workflow completed, first ticket resolved under SLA — are flagged as product-qualified leads. The CEO Agent identifies the ones who are on Core tier but are using Plus-tier behaviors. The upgrade prompt goes out automatically, but it's human in tone: "You've automated 12 workflows this month — that's Plus tier behavior. Want to unlock the full feature set?"

**3. Partner Channel**

Agencies, fractional operators, startup consultants, and SaaS tools with adjacent user bases all become distribution. The incentive model: revenue share for referrals, early API access for integrations, co-marketing for case studies.

**4. Outbound**

The CEO Agent identifies companies that match the ICP: startup or growth-stage, 5–100 employees, using a known fragmented stack (HubSpot + Intercom + Notion + Linear is the tell). The Growth Agent writes personalized first-touch emails — specific to the company's stack, size, and industry. Not a mass blast. Targeted, relevant, worth reading.

**5. SEO Dominance**

By Month 22, the content library has 150+ articles covering every CRM keyword a startup founder would search. The SEO flywheel is running. The goal is category ownership — when a founder searches "CRM for SaaS startup," the first three results should all lead back to ellocharlie content.

## The 7% Formula

What does 7% week-over-week actually require?

| Customer Count | New Customers Needed per Week | New Customers Needed per Day | Frequency |
|----------------|------------------------------|------------------------------|-----------|
| 2 | 0.1 | — | 1 every 50 days |
| 10 | 0.7 | — | 1 every 10 days |
| 50 | 3.5 | 0.5 | 1 every 2 days |
| 100 | 7.0 | 1.0 | 1 per day |
| 500 | 35.0 | 5.0 | 5 per day |
| 1,000 | 70.0 | 10.0 | 10 per day |

The shift that happens between 50 and 100 customers is the critical one. At 50 customers, you can still close every new customer personally. At 100 customers, you need one new customer every single day — and the self-serve motion has to be working.

This is why the Phase 2 work — the content flywheel, the onboarding automation, the partner channel — must begin in earnest by Week 24, even though the immediate growth need doesn't require it. The content published at Week 30 ranks at Week 60. The automation built at Week 30 converts at Week 50. You're always building for the phase after the one you're in.

The 7% formula is not a passive target. It is a weekly forcing function. If the number is off by even one week, the compounding falls behind. This is why the CEO Agent tracks it daily.

## Key Metrics Dashboard

The CEO Agent tracks the following every day. These are not vanity metrics. They are the operational variables of the growth engine.

| Metric | Definition | Target | Red Flag |
|--------|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Weekly Active Customers** | Customers with at least one meaningful product action in the last 7 days | >90% of paying customers | <80% |
| **Week-over-Week Growth Rate** | (Customers this week − Customers last week) / Customers last week | 7.0% | <5.0% |
| **MRR** | Monthly Recurring Revenue | Per model | Any decline |
| **ARR** | MRR × 12 | Per model | — |
| **Net MRR Growth** | New MRR + Expansion MRR − Churned MRR | Positive | Negative |
| **CAC** | Total acquisition spend / New customers acquired | <$500 Phase 1, <$1,000 Phase 3 | >2× target |
| **LTV** | Avg MRR per customer × Avg customer lifespan | >$5,000 at blended pricing | <$2,000 |
| **LTV:CAC Ratio** | LTV / CAC | >3× | <2× |
| **Time to Value** | Hours from signup to first activation milestone | <24 hours | >72 hours |
| **NPS** | Net Promoter Score (quarterly survey) | >70 | <50 |
| **Monthly Churn Rate** | Churned customers / Total customers at start of month | <2% | >3% |
| **First Response Time** | Time from ticket open to first agent response | <15 minutes | >1 hour |
| **Trial-to-Paid Rate** | % of trial users who convert to paying | >20% | <12% |
| **Expansion Revenue %** | MRR from upgrades / Total MRR | >15% | <5% |

**The CEO Agent's daily summary includes:** current customer count, WoW growth rate vs. target, any accounts that went dark in the last 48 hours, any response time SLA breaches, and any churn events from the previous 7 days.

Nothing should surprise us. That's the point.

### Hiring Philosophy

We don't hire to fill roles. We hire when an agent hits its ceiling and the gap is something only a human can fill.

**Human #3 — Head of Customer Success (~$25K MRR / Month 17)**
At ~186 customers, the CX Lead Agent is handling the majority of support volume effectively. But the strategic layer — managing at-risk accounts, running quarterly business reviews, building the reference customer program — requires human judgment and relationship equity. This person is not a support rep. They're a relationship manager who uses the CX Agent as their primary tool.

**Human #4 — Growth / Content Lead (~$50K MRR / Month 19)**
At ~366 customers, the content flywheel needs a human curator. The Growth Agent produces; the Content Lead edits, prioritizes, and builds the editorial calendar. This person also owns the partner program, the case study pipeline, and the referral mechanics.

**Human #5 — Product Engineer (~$100K MRR / Month 22)**
At ~771 customers, the product surface area is large enough that the CTO Agent needs a human partner who can ship independently. This engineer works in close loop with Nicholas on roadmap execution.

Every hire after Human #5 follows the same logic: what is the agent failing at, and does that failure require a human?

## The Underlying Theory

The 7% growth target is chosen deliberately. It is aggressive enough to create urgency every week without being impossible. It compounds to a trajectory that reaches $10M ARR in under three years from a two-customer start.

But the number matters less than the discipline it represents. Seven percent means something went wrong if you miss it. It means every week you have a conversation with the growth rate before you have a conversation with anything else. It means the growth function is not an abstraction — it's the number on the whiteboard.

The channels will shift. The tactics will evolve. What the customer looks like in Month 24 is not what they look like in Month 3. But the underlying mechanism — show up for every customer, close the loop between sales and support, make the product earn the renewal — that doesn't change.

Paul Graham said do things that don't scale. We're doing that. We're also building the systems that let us stop doing them at exactly the right moment, and not a week before.
